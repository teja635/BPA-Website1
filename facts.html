Types of Drugs

Central Nervous System Depressants

CNS depressants are most commonly known as sedatives as their main function is to combat anxiety and sleep disorders. Although their intent is not harmful, their effects on the body are highly addictive and have been abused by people since the early days of the drug. Benzodiazepines and Barbiturates are the two main categories of the CNS depressants that cause addiction and are thus physicians limit their use of the two categories of the drug to short term use only as long term use can cause the patient to be dependent and addicted to the drug. Commonly known CNS depressants include diazepam(valium), alprazolam(xanex) and mephobarbital(Mebaral).

Central Nervous System Stimulants

Central Nervous System stimulants are by far the most commonly used drugs and include Amphetamine, cocaine, and methamphetamine. They work by stimulating the release of neurotransmitters associated with reward and pleasure and give users feelings of increased energy, attention, heart rate, confidence, and increased respiratory rates leading the abuser to feel invincible. Common side effects of CNS simulates include lack of appetite, mood swings, depersonalization, weight loss, headaches, irritability, panic attacks, depression, and paranoia. Prolonged usage of CNS stimulants distances the user from reality gives them severe suicidal thoughts. As these drugs are used on multiple occasions, the liver will produce more and more soft endoplasmic rictulum to compensate for the extenuating amount of detoxifying that is required from the liver and thus the individual will have to take larger and larger doses of the drug in order to obtain that feeling that they first felt and in doing so increase the amount of damage they do to their body. Escaping the detrimental chains put forth by an individual’s intake of CNS stimulants is hard and long as the body will be accustomed to the high amount of reward neurotransmitters released by the brain when the drug is taken.

Hallucinogenics(LSD) & Dissociative anesthetics(PCP)

Hallucinogenics, unlike the other drugs described, do not have as much of a detrimental effect on the body but rather pose as a health risk as they impair judgement and cause individuals see images and hear sounds that are non-existant. Hallucinogenics commonly effect the body by stimulating the sporadic release of the neurotransmitter serotonin. Research is still being done on the effects of hallucinogenics but current research supports the theory that they obscuring communication between the neurotransmitter systems that exist in the brain and spinal cord to regulate behavior and cause users to experience a feeling of euphoria and see and hear images and sounds that are nonexistent. Dissociative anesthetics like PCP take the effects of hallucinogenics and go a step further by making the user feel out of control and disconnected from themselves and the world around them. The effects of dissociative anesthetics cause symptoms comparable to those paranoid schizophrenia and when taken in higher doses can lead to respiratory distress and can lead to death.

Narcotic Analgesics(opioids)

Narcotic Analgesics are commonly taken to cope with pain. There effects are similar to that of Non-steroidal anti inflammatory drugs i.e. advil but their effects are far more apparent. Specialists say that opioids can be taken by individuals as long as they are taken in moderation. This simple restriction is commonly ignored as the more opioid an individual takes the more pain relief they experience and this can in most cases have a cascading effect. The main problem with opioids is that they depress respiratory rates. When taken in large doses, an individual’s respiratory rate may slow down so much to the point of suffocation. This is the problem with most opioids as people who take the drug without adequate supervision by professionals may end up overdosing. Opioids work by acting on receptors located on neuronal cell membranes. By doing this they relieve pain signals coming from the peripheral nervous system to the brain. People suffering from chronic pain are more likely to develop dependence on opioids for pain relief. Professionals say that dependence of the drug is safe as long as the use is taking the allocated amount that they are supposed to. Tolerance, unlike with other drugs, is not a problem as long as the drug is not taken in exorbitant doses. The best action to prevent these drugs from causing damage is to listen to a professional supervisor on how much you should take and do not take more than the allowed amount you are prescribed.

Inhalants

Inhalants hide in plain sight in a common household in the from of cleaning substances, and paint. Individuals can get high from these substances from inhaling the fumes from the substances. Inhaling such substances slows down brain activity or expands vessels and by doing so creates the high that people strive for. The high from these substances is short lived and thus it is common for users to inhale repeatedly. The repetitive inhalation of these substances can cause someone to lose consciousness and even die. Long term effects include damage to nerve fibers and damage to brain cells. Death from inhalants can be attributed to a multitude of causes including cardiac arrest, asphyxiation, suffocation, convolutions, seizures, coma, choking, and injuries due to lack of judgement. Due to their overbearing prevalence in a common household, the most common type of abuser are middle school students who have such substances readily available to them.
